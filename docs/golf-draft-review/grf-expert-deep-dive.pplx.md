## 2. Dana Dahlquist — Sway Gap, Horizontal-Vertical Balance, Coaching Transparency

### 2.1 Dahlquist's Core GRF Philosophy

Dana Dahlquist's coaching identity is at the intersection of biomechanical precision and practical simplicity. He originally taught conventional grip/aim/posture fundamentals, but his work with Josh Koch (long drive) and then Bryson DeChambeau forced him into a much deeper kinetic-kinematic synthesis ([Avidgolfer Magazine, 2026](https://myavidgolfer.com/golf-science-an-inside-look-at-coaching/)).

His GRF philosophy has three core planks:

**1. Geometry first, forces second.**
Dahlquist always starts with the geometry of the swing — where the club is, where the body is, what the attack angle and swing direction are — and then uses force data to explain *why* the geometry looks the way it does. Force plates confirm or complicate the kinematic story; they do not replace it. This aligns with the TrackMan 3D-first approach at Forefront.

**2. The 50-50 horizontal-vertical rule for speed.**
For distance players, Dahlquist explicitly targets a 50/50 balance between horizontal and vertical forces ([Avidgolfer, 2026](https://myavidgolfer.com/golf-science-an-inside-look-at-coaching/)). He views verticals as the primary speed source ("the most important for speed development") but recognizes that purely vertical motion tends to produce hooks — the clubface closes more aggressively without the lateral-braking component to time rotation. His target is: enough vertical to create the extended-lever/post-up effect, but enough horizontal braking to stabilize path and face timing. **Forefront parallel:** this directly supports the kinetic sequence model; a player strong in vertical but weak in torque/braking (Too-Much-Vertical fault pattern) benefits from Dahlquist's 50/50 framing.

**3. Braking as the speed multiplier.**
Dahlquist explicitly states: *"the better the brakes and the more force you put in, the more speed you can generate"* ([Avidgolfer, 2026](https://myavidgolfer.com/golf-science-an-inside-look-at-coaching/)). This is consistent with Dr. Scott Lynn's data — braking force (negative lateral spike) is the mechanism that converts linear COM momentum into rotational acceleration. Charles Howell III's historic braking force (37% BW in the negative lateral on Swing Catalyst) is the extreme example. Dahlquist integrates this into lesson language by reframing it not as "slowing down" but as "creating better torque."

### 2.2 The Sway Gap — Dahlquist's Kinematic GRF Proxy

The **sway gap** is Dahlquist's term for the horizontal distance between the chest and pelvis, measured face-on at key P-positions. It is his primary kinematic GRF-proxy metric.

**Why sway gap works as a proxy:**
When the pelvis moves aggressively toward the target (forward lateral force) and the chest stays back (creating separation/tilt), the sway gap opens — wider gap = more COP-to-COM separation = longer moment arm = more rotational torque available. When both chest and pelvis move together (no gap), the COM shifts without creating a torque lever, mimicking a sway or slide fault pattern.

| Sway Gap State | Biomechanical Meaning | Observable Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| **Gap opening (pelvis forward, chest back)** | Lead-foot braking occurring; vertical force building; rotation potential increasing | Shoulder tilt increases; club shallows; impact tilt right |
| **Gap staying narrow** | Pelvis and chest moving together; no braking; linear momentum not converting to rotation | Steep angle of attack; open face at impact; slide/block pattern |
| **Gap collapsing (chest catches up too early)** | Rotation complete before impact; deceleration early; low low-point stability | Over-rotation; flipping; high spin |

**[B] Dahlquist-DeChambeau application:** Dahlquist tracks rib-cage rotation, side-bend, sway gap, and pelvic lift-and-lower for Bryson to confirm that the ranges match consistently with the speed input. He uses Sportsbox AI to verify these parameters shot by shot ([Avidgolfer, 2026](https://myavidgolfer.com/golf-science-an-inside-look-at-coaching/)).

**Forefront application:** TrackMan 3D captures pelvis and thorax position at automated P-positions. The **pelvis-thorax displacement delta** (lateral or tilted) at P5–P7 is the TrackMan 3D analog of Dahlquist's sway gap. Coaches can read this without a force plate by comparing pelvis lateral displacement vs. thorax position in the TrackMan 3D body frame. A player with early extension will show pelvis-COM moving toward the ball (gap collapsing forward) rather than pelvis leading left with chest staying back.

### 2.3 Dahlquist on OTT / Steep Delivery — Vertical Force as the Fix

One of Dahlquist's most operationally useful insights is his approach to steep/OTT patterns: **increasing vertical force in the backswing** (not just the downswing) to improve rotation and shallow the delivery ([YouTube 2024, "Stop Swinging Over the Top By Using More Vertical Force"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SmDq2I8htNY)).

His logic chain:
1. OTT players are typically under-rotating the trail hip in the backswing
2. If the trail hip rotates on an incline (vertical component added to rotation), the club naturally gets deeper behind the player
3. This creates a stretch through the obliques and into the chest that loads the thorax-pelvis separation
4. From that loaded position, the downswing naturally shallows because the delivery path is already shifted

**Coaching cue:** Rather than telling the player to "shallow the club" (internal/positional), Dahlquist cues: *"Feel that elevation, that stretch and height on your trail hip as you turn back."* This is an external focus on a physical sensation rather than a club-position instruction. He then has the player pause at the top for three seconds (removing momentum-driven compensation), then lead with the lower body to initiate the downswing — a direct motor learning application.

**Drill:** Pause drill — turn to the top, hold three seconds, then allow lower body to sequence first. Removes upper-body dominance at transition without a technical verbal cue about the arms or club. ([YouTube 2024](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SmDq2I8htNY))

### 2.4 Dahlquist's Rotation Communication — "Three Directions"

Dahlquist explicitly teaches rotation as a **three-dimensional concept**, not a single horizontal plane rotation ([YouTube 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=konOHWTujaA)):

> *"Think of rotation not only working around but also vertically... there's a term they call frontal plane torque — that's what that means. It's not necessarily horizontal this way."*

This is a direct match with Kwon's GRF moment arm model: true pelvis rotation is not purely transverse; it includes frontal plane torque (the tilt/push that creates the long moment arm). When Dahlquist cues "land pressure left, closed, hips forward, chest down," he is physically describing:
- Lead foot absorbing weight (vertical GRF building)
- Pelvis moved forward without thorax (sway gap opening = moment arm extending)
- Chest remaining over ball (lead arm parallel timing maintained)

This three-directional rotation concept is important for Forefront communication because many clients arrive with "rotation" in their vocabulary meaning only horizontal/transverse motion. Redefining rotation as three-directional early in the coaching relationship prevents the "I'm rotating but not getting power" confusion.

### 2.5 Dahlquist's Coaching Philosophy and Communication Style

**Transparency with professionals:** Dahlquist's operating rule is complete transparency — say what you know, acknowledge what you don't ([Avidgolfer, 2026](https://myavidgolfer.com/golf-science-an-inside-look-at-coaching/)). For Tour players, the relationship is built on confidence: the player must trust that the coach has seen the data, understands the geometry, and is not guessing. This aligns with Forefront's evidence-tagged communication standard.

**Mixed-population communication:** Dahlquist credits teaching a wide mix (juniors through Tour) for sharpening his communication. Each client's vocabulary requires recalibration. He moves from complex biomechanical explanations to simple feel-based cues based on the player's processing style. This is exactly the Winkelman DESCRIBE → CUE transition operationalized in practice.

**Predictability as the north star:** For Tour players, Dahlquist frames the goal as "predictability — a particular outcome that matches their visualization." This is a goal-setting / process-orientation principle that aligns with SDT (self-determination theory) and motor learning transfer research. The swing does not need to look like Rory McIlroy's; it needs to produce the same ball flight on demand. This reframes GRF work for the client: *"We are not building a force plate pattern — we are building a reliable kinematic pattern that produces predictable force and predictable ball flight."*

## 3. Phil Cheetham / GEARS — Kinematic Sequence as GRF Proxy: Numbers, Gain Factors, Biofeedback

### 3.1 The Kinematic Sequence: Quantified Reference Values

Phil Cheetham's primary contribution to golf biomechanics is the operationalization of the **kinematic sequence** — the rotational speed graph of body segments through the downswing. GEARS / AMM3D and now Sportsbox 3D expose this data. TrackMan 3D provides segment positions that, combined with velocity calculations, approximate the same information.

**Tour reference values (Cheetham's AMM3D database, ~100 tour pros, male)** ([Golf Science Lab 2017](https://golfwell.co/kinematic-sequence/); [Sportsbox KS webinar 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v1rj52nVM8s)):

| Segment | Peak Rotation Speed (deg/sec) | Timing of Peak (relative to impact) | Pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pelvis** | ~600–750 deg/sec | ~arm parallel in downswing | Peaks first; decelerates before impact |
| **Thorax** | ~800–1000 deg/sec | After pelvis peak | Peaks second; decelerates before impact |
| **Lead Arm** | ~1000–1300 deg/sec | After thorax peak | Peaks third |
| **Club** | ~2000–2400 deg/sec | At or just before impact | Peaks last and highest |

**Gain factors** (Cheetham's term): The ratio of each segment's peak speed to the previous segment's peak speed. Tour average across the thorax-to-arm and arm-to-club transitions is approximately **1.5×** ([Sportsbox KS webinar 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v1rj52nVM8s)). A gain factor below 1.5 at any joint indicates energy is being lost at that joint — it is not amplifying; it is absorbing.

| Gain Factor Location | Tour Average | Below-Average Interpretation | Coaching Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pelvis → Thorax | ~1.3–1.5× | Core/oblique weakness; insufficient X-Factor Stretch | Address with rotational power training; X-Factor Stretch drill |
| Thorax → Lead Arm | ~1.4–1.6× | Arm deceleration mismatch; timing error | Check sequence timing; internal rotation drills |
| Lead Arm → Club | ~1.5–2.0× | Late release; handle drag; insufficient wrist speed | Release timing drills; grip tension check |

**Why this is a GRF proxy:** The sequence requires that each upstream segment first accelerates, then *decelerates* before the next segment peaks. That deceleration happens because the interaction force between segments — which is driven by GRF — transfers energy forward. A player without adequate GRF production shows **flat gain factors** (close to 1.0) or **reversed sequence** (thorax peaking before pelvis). These patterns are visible in Sportsbox 3D or TrackMan 3D without a force plate.

**[B] Cheetham consensus data:** 60% of tour pros sequence perfectly (pelvis → thorax → arm → club in that order); 74% have at least pelvis-before-thorax; 89% initiate with pelvis first at transition ([Golf Science Lab 2017](https://golfwell.co/kinematic-sequence/)). Any client who initiates with the thorax before the pelvis is in the bottom 11% of sequencing — a significant finding regardless of handicap.

### 3.2 The Linear Kinematic Sequence

Cheetham also identified a **linear kinematic sequence** that parallels the rotational one — the sequence of peak *linear* (translational) speed of the lead hip, lead shoulder, hands, and clubhead ([TPI, Cheetham — "The Linear Kinematic Sequence"](https://www.mytpi.com/articles/biomechanics/the-linear-kinematic-sequence)):

| Segment | Linear Peak Speed | Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Lead hip | Peaks first; typically ~22 mph | Decelerates before impact |
| Lead shoulder | Peaks second | Decelerates before impact |
| Mid-hands | ~22 mph average (tour); peaks third | Decelerates before impact |
| Clubhead | ~110+ mph; peaks at/near impact | Accelerates through impact |

**Key insight:** The mid-hands average *peak* speed of 22 mph occurs *before* impact; at impact they have decelerated to ~17.6 mph. This means hands that are still accelerating at impact are actually an **efficiency problem** — they have not yet passed their energy forward to the club. This is the mechanism behind "trying too hard with the hands at impact" reducing CHS. **Forefront coaching application:** when a client reports that hitting harder with the hands feels like it should go farther, this data explains why it doesn't. The hands must decelerate for the club to accelerate.

### 3.3 X-Factor Stretch — The GRF Loading Event

The **X-Factor Stretch** is Cheetham's most cited original research finding. It is not the X-Factor itself (peak shoulder-hip differential at the top) but the *increase* in X-Factor between the top of the backswing and the early downswing ([GEARS / AMM3D research](https://www.philcheetham.com/workshop/)).

**Mechanism:** As the pelvis initiates transition and begins moving toward the target (early lateral GRF from trail foot → reaction toward target), the thorax and shoulders briefly *lag behind* or even continue rotating in the backswing direction. This momentarily increases thorax-pelvis separation. The elastic/contractile tension created in the trunk musculature during this stretch is then rapidly released, driving the thorax-to-arm gain factor.

**GRF connection:** Without adequate GRF driving the pelvis into transition first, there is no X-Factor Stretch — the thorax cannot lag if the pelvis never leads. Players who initiate with the shoulders (OTT pattern) show reduced or absent X-Factor Stretch, reduced thorax-pelvis gain factor, and typically lower kinetic sequence order correlation with tour data.

**Observable proxy (no force plate, no 3D):** In face-on video at early transition (P4→P4.5), the lead hip should be visibly moving toward the target while the shoulders are still completing their backswing direction or at least not yet turning toward the target. The "reversal" of the hip before the shoulder is the X-Factor Stretch in action.

### 3.4 Biofeedback Drills for Kinematic Sequence

Cheetham pioneered using real-time auditory and visual biofeedback to change kinematic sequence ([Cheetham blog, 2009](http://philcheethamthe3dguy.blogspot.com/2009/12/changing-kinematic-sequence-with.html)). Sportsbox now does this automatically with its KS gain factor indicators.

**Biofeedback drill protocol:**
1. Establish a baseline KS trace (Sportsbox or TrackMan 3D)
2. Identify the *first* sequencing error (e.g., thorax peaks before pelvis)
3. Prescribe a constraint drill that forces the pelvis to move first
4. Hit 10 swings with real-time KS indicator visible; observe if thorax-pelvis sequence corrects
5. If corrected: test transfer (remove feedback, hit 5 shots, compare KS)
6. If not corrected: check physical capacity (hip rotation mobility, T-spine mobility)

**Forefront parallel:** TrackMan 3D's AI motion analysis detects sequence flags automatically. The Sportsbox "Gain Factor" screen provides the same data in a client-facing visual format. This is the practice tool for GP-L 2–3 players working on kinematic sequence without a force plate.

### 3.5 Cheetham on Rotation-Dominant vs. Lateral-Dominant Player Types

Cheetham's GEARS database distinguishes players whose **primary speed gain comes from rotation** (thorax-pelvis gain is high) vs. those who gain primarily from **lateral/translational mechanics** ([Sportsbox KS webinar 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v1rj52nVM8s)). This maps directly onto Lynn's H/T/V dominant classification:
- High rotational gain factor → Torque/Spinner archetype (Lynn)
- High lateral COM contribution to sequence → Horizontal/Glider archetype (Lynn/Adams)
- High gain factor at the arm-club joint from post-up → Vertical/Launcher archetype

**Forefront integration:** At intake, use Sportsbox or TrackMan 3D to identify the player's gain factor profile. Cross-reference with Lynn's H/T/V classification when force plate data is available. These two data layers confirm each other — or reveal a mismatch that needs resolution.

## 4. Mike Adams / BioSwing Dynamics — Glider/Spinner/Launcher Classification and Foot-Stance Tuning

### 4.1 The Three-Archetype Model: Glider / Spinner / Launcher

Mike Adams and Scott Lynn jointly developed the terminology, from Lynn's PING study data, for three dominant GRF archetypes ([Mike Adams / Scott Lynn BioSwing Webinar 2022](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xoiW3j2ehNA); [Adams YouTube 2022](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4UUMZy6xbVg)):

| Archetype | Primary GRF Force | Kinetic Sequence Dominance | Body Movement Pattern | Tour Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Glider** | Horizontal (lateral) | Horizontal peaks large; torque moderate | Moves side-to-side with COM shift | DJ, Bryson DeChambeau, right-leg dominant players |
| **Spinner** | Torque (rotational / frontal plane) | Torque peaks large; horizontal moderate | Rotates in place with minimal COM shift | Matt Kuchar, Charles Howell III, left-leg dominant players |
| **Launcher** | Vertical (sagittal) | Vertical peaks large; lead-leg extension dominant | Jump/post-up prominent | Lexi Thompson, Justin Thomas, front-post pivot |

**Important nuance:** Adams is careful to state that every golfer uses all three forces; the archetype reflects which force is *dominant* for that player ([Adams YouTube 2022](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4UUMZy6xbVg)). The archetype is not a flaw — it is a pattern to optimize, not eliminate.

**[B] PING Study summary (Lynn):** Of ~969 driver swings measured, 53% were Horizontal dominant, 36% Vertical dominant, 11% Torque dominant. Amateur distribution (by shaft flex) shows underrepresentation of Torque — Torque is the hardest force to train and the most absent in unskilled players.

### 4.2 Post-Type: Front Post vs. Rear Post

Adams integrates a "pivot post" concept alongside the force archetype ([Scott Lynn, Swing Catalyst 2021](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_WZVQtk2OoM)):

| Post Type | Primary Pivot Leg | Primary Force Association | Optimal Stance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Front Post** | Lead leg | Vertical / Launcher | Closer stance; earlier vertical force timing |
| **Rear Post** | Trail leg | Horizontal / Glider | Wider stance; larger lateral force |
| **Center Post** | Bilateral | Torque / Spinner | Medium stance; AP force couple balanced |

**How to determine post type:** Jump testing (Smart2Move or jump mat). Observe which leg the player naturally pivots around in lateral jumps. Lynn's cluster analysis on the Swing Catalyst also identifies dominant leg from force plate data ([Swing Catalyst 2021](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_WZVQtk2OoM)). An alternative field-test: have the player stand on one leg and simulate the downswing hip motion — the leg that feels more natural as an anchor is typically the dominant/post leg.

**Matching swing geometry to post type:** Adams makes an explicit claim that force-type and swing plane are coupled ([Scott Lynn YouTube 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v5PD-KqemVs)):
- **Gliders** (right-leg dominant, high horizontal): benefit from higher hand plane (hands reaching toward the sky vs. the wall behind); path tends to be slightly inside-out
- **Spinners** (torque dominant, center or left-leg): benefit from deeper hands (reaching to the wall); path tends leftward
- **Launchers** (vertical dominant, lead-leg): benefit from moderately high hands; tend to have steeper attack angle

This is a significant integration point: a player's GRF archetype should match their hand plane and attack angle. Prescribing a high hand plane to a Spinner, or a deep/flat hand plane to a Glider, creates a mechanical mismatch that GRF training alone cannot resolve.

### 4.3 Foot and Stance Adjustments as the GRF Tuning Tool

Adams' most practically operational insight is that **feet and stance width directly control the timing and magnitude of all three GRF components** — without requiring any conscious force production change from the player ([Adams YouTube 2022](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4UUMZy6xbVg)):

| Foot/Stance Adjustment | Effect on GRF |
|---|---|
| **Flaring the lead foot out** | Lead leg breaks later → vertical force peaks later; torque peaks later |
| **Squaring the lead foot** | Lead leg breaks sooner → vertical force peaks earlier; torque peaks sooner |
| **Dropping the trail foot back (closed stance)** | Lead leg locks up sooner → earlier vertical peak |
| **Opening stance** | Lead leg locks up later → later vertical peak |
| **Widening stance** | More horizontal force (glide); more stability for lateral COM shift |
| **Narrowing stance** | More torque and vertical; less lateral range |

**Practical application:** If a player's vertical force is peaking too late (confirmed by force plate or inferred from late timing on TrackMan 3D body data), try squaring the lead foot slightly before adding any drill or cue. The foot adjustment alone may correct the timing. This is Adams' "measure not guess" principle — test foot position changes on the force plate before prescribing drill interventions.

**Forefront protocol:** In the absence of a real-time force plate, use TrackMan 3D's pelvis velocity curve and the timing of pelvis deceleration as the proxy metric. If pelvis deceleration (the sign that it is transferring energy to the thorax) occurs late, try squaring the lead foot by 5–10°, re-test 5 shots, compare.

### 4.4 Kinetic Sequence Windows — Adams/Lynn Timing Benchmarks

Adams (working from Lynn's data) specifies **timing windows** for each force component peak relative to the downswing ([Adams YouTube 2022](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4UUMZy6xbVg)):

| GRF Component | Peak Timing Window |
|---|---|
| **Horizontal (Lateral)** | Between top of backswing and lead arm at 45° (L-arm at 45°) |
| **Torque (Rotational)** | Between lead arm at 45° and lead arm parallel to ground (L-arm ∥) |
| **Vertical** | Between lead arm parallel and shaft parallel |

**Diagnosis:** If any peak falls outside its window (e.g., horizontal doesn't peak until lead arm parallel — it is late; or vertical peaks before lead arm parallel — it is early), efficiency is lost. Adams demonstrates this with Swing Catalyst plates showing that re-sequencing to correct windows produces immediate CHS gains without any other change.

## 5. Dr. Scott Lynn / Swing Catalyst — Dominant Force Type, Kinetic Sequence Windows, Leg Dominance

### 5.1 The PING Study — Most-Cited GRF Research in Golf Instruction

Lynn's PING study (conducted with the PING engineering team, presented multiple times at Swing Catalyst and PGA events, [Carolinas PGA 2019](https://carolinas.pga.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/3/2019/03/Dr.-Scott-Lynn-PhD-Swing-Catalyst.pdf)) remains the most-cited dataset for tour-level GRF benchmarks.

**Key PING Study findings:**

| Metric | Tour Average | SD |
|---|---|---|
| Peak Vertical GRF | 179% BW | ± 20% |
| Peak Torque (frontal plane) | 127.7 Nm | ± 16 Nm |
| Peak Horizontal GRF | 19.0% BW | ± 4.1% |

**Dominant force type distribution (driver swings):**
- Horizontal dominant: 513 swings (53%)
- Vertical dominant: 351 swings (36%)
- Torque dominant: 105 swings (11%)

**[C] Review required:** Torque-dominant players show a strong association with a more leftward swing direction (out-to-in path tendency). Prescribing torque training for a player who already swings left can worsen the path. Lynn's recommendation: for Torque-dominant players with a leftward path, address grip first (stronger grip to offset face-opening from rotation) before adding torque volume.

### 5.2 Leg Dominance and Force Type — The Missing Link

Lynn's cluster analysis identified that **leg dominance is the single strongest predictor of force type** ([Swing Catalyst 2021](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_WZVQtk2OoM)):

- **Right-leg dominant (rear post):** Horizontal force production is natural; tend to be Gliders
- **Left-leg dominant (front post):** Vertical force production is natural; tend to be Launchers
- **Bilateral (neither dominant):** Torque force is the residual when neither lateral shift nor vertical jump dominates; tend to be Spinners

**How to identify leg dominance without force plates:**
1. Have the player jump laterally off each leg. Which direction is more comfortable?
2. Watch the backswing. Does the pelvis rotate around the trail hip (rear post) or does it drift and then return (front post)?
3. Use Smart2Move / Swing Catalyst jump cluster analysis if available

**[B]** Players who try to use the ground in a pattern that is *opposite* to their natural leg dominance will feel uncoordinated, show poor timing, and often produce compensatory upper-body movements. The prescription is to first identify, then optimize within type — not override to produce a single universal pattern.

### 5.3 Lynn on Torque Training — The Most Trainable Gap

Lynn explicitly states that **Torque is the force type that is most absent in amateur players and the hardest to train**, based on the PING study amateur data showing torque-dominant swings are extremely rare in R/S/X flex shaft golfers ([Scott Lynn YouTube 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v5PD-KqemVs)).

**Why amateurs under-produce torque:**
- Torque requires the lead foot to push *toward the ball* (A/P force) while the trail foot pushes *away from the ball* — a simultaneous force couple
- This requires bilateral foot action coordination that does not occur without deliberate training
- Visual observation: players with weak torque tend to push both feet in the same direction (bilateral lateral) rather than in opposing A/P directions

**Torque-building drill — Scott Lynn:** Place the lead foot on the ground and actively push the forefoot of the lead foot *toward the ball* (anterior direction) while pushing the trail foot *away from the ball*. This creates the AP force couple. Externalize: "Spread the ground with your feet" (Winkelman-style) or "Push the floor apart" (opposing directions).

## 6. Josh Koch — Speed-Out-In-Front, Block Practice, Learning Philosophy

### 6.1 Koch's GRF Philosophy: "Pressure to the Lead Side Without Moving Too Much Mass"

Koch's contribution to GRF coaching is primarily at the **intent and moment arm level**, not the technical force measurement level. His operational concept is ([Josh Koch YouTube 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UDj2TGa42PI)):

> *"The trick is to get pressure to the lead side without moving too much mass to the lead — that creates what we call moment arm."*

He uses the **wrench analogy**: a longer wrench creates more torque on a stuck nut. A golfer who gets lead-side pressure without lateral mass drift creates a longer moment arm — the COP-COM separation that Kwon's GRF moment math requires. This is entirely consistent with AMG's torque-leverage visualization and Kwon's moment arm geometry, but expressed in language that players immediately grasp.

Koch's drill application: trail foot on an upslope, swing back and feel the tilt/wind; then flip and go uphill, land left, use the lead leg to post up. The slope constraint forces the lead-leg vertical extension without coaching the extension directly.

### 6.2 Mach 3 Protocol — Learning Philosophy and Block Practice Architecture

Koch explicitly credits James Sieckman (short game) for introducing him to **block practice** as a skill-building method ([Koch YouTube 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UDj2TGa42PI)):

> *"Block practice was a concept introduced to me by James Sieckman, who's a [leading short game instructor]... I would do five reps of hitting balls and then five reps of a drill, and cycle back and forth."*

This is the Forefront model's blocked-to-random progression at work. Koch's operationalization:
- **Block phase:** 5 Mach 3 speed swings (concept/sub-max protocol) → immediate proprioceptive feedback
- **Integration phase:** 5 full swings at normal intent
- **Repeat:** do not do 50 block swings in a row — the skill pattern washes out

Koch also distinguishes **skill work vs. speed work** — recognizing that these require different practice modes:
> *"The practice of hitting balls hard teaches you to shut your brain off... but at some point you've got to make some Mechanicals."*

**Forefront translation:** Speed sessions (Mach 3) are not the same as technical GRF sessions. In a single practice day:
- Technical GRF block: constraint drill (external focus) → TrackMan 3D confirmation → 10 shots
- Speed block: Mach 3 sub-max protocol → launch monitor CHS monitoring → not mixing with GRF technical analysis during the block

### 6.3 Koch on Warm-Up and Nervous System Readiness

Koch's warm-up before speed sessions emphasizes hip activation and T-spine rotation — "pigeon stretch, activating glutes, getting some T-spine rotation" ([Koch YouTube 2023](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UDj2TGa42PI)). This maps directly to Forefront's warmup block architecture from the RIPA framework (Reactive, Inhibitory, Pattern-based, Activating). The difference in Koch's protocol vs. a generic warmup is the progressive speed ramp:

1. 10 balls at moderate speed (shaking out the pattern)
2. 10 balls at sub-max speed (Mach 3 concept: speed out in front)
3. 10 balls at max intent

He explicitly notes that he does not go straight to 100% — the speed build is gradual and measured with a launch monitor on every swing.

### 6.4 The Koch-Dahlquist Collaboration — Integration of Biomechanics and Speed

Josh Koch works directly alongside Dana Dahlquist ([HitBombs.com](https://www.hitbombs.com)). Their combined model is:
- Dahlquist handles swing geometry, force balance (horizontal-vertical), kinematic framing, and use of Sportsbox/TrackMan/GC Quad
- Koch handles speed expression, Mach 3 protocol, nervous system loading, intent-transfer drills

This is the closest thing in the coaching world to the Forefront model of separating **technique development** (GP-L 1–3) from **speed expression** (GP-L 4). The two coaches do not blur these functions — they are distinct roles in the same team.

## 7. Chris Como — AMTI Lab, GASP Software, Moment Arm Geometry, Kwon-Como Pivot

### 7.1 The AMTI Lab Setup

Como operates a dual-plate AMTI AccuGait-Optimized (ACG-O) 50×50 cm force plate system in his personal lab, fed into GASP Systems Ltd. software ([AMTI, 2025](https://www.amti.biz/2025/05/14/amti-user-spotlight-chris-comos-golf-swing-biomechanics-lab-in-his-own-living-room/)). GASP (Golf Analysis Software Program) was co-developed with Dr. Kwon and provides every GRF parameter relevant to the golf swing:

| GASP Parameter | What It Measures | Coaching Value |
|---|---|---|
| Vertical Force (Fz, per foot) | BW% loading through each foot over time | SSC timing; peak magnitude; unweighting detection |
| Horizontal Trail/Lead Force | Lateral (right-left) GRF per foot | Double spike timing; braking force magnitude |
| Horizontal Anterior/Posterior Force | Forward-backward force per foot | AP force couple; OTT diagnosis; torque building |
| Center of Pressure | COP position per foot and combined | Trace pattern; loading rhythm |
| COP Velocity | Speed of COP movement | Transition quickness; dynamic trigger quality |
| Center of Mass | Estimated from kinematic + kinetic data | COM-COP gap = moment arm basis |
| Moment Arm | Distance from combined GRF vector to COM | Key efficiency variable; Kwon's primary metric |
| Frontal Plane Torque | Transverse-axis rotation moment | Moment driving downswing rotation |
| Sagittal Plane Torque | Forward-backward axis moment | Lead-side bracing mechanism |
| Stance Width | Measured foot-to-foot | Biomechanical baseline |

**[B]** Como's lab allows him to build normative data for his client pool and compare swing changes against force data, not just kinematic data. This is the highest resolution coaching environment in practical use (outside university labs).

### 7.2 The Kwon-Como Pivot — Operationalized

The Kwon-Como pivot is a sequential stepping pattern that naturally produces optimal GRF geometry ([Newton Golf Institute, 2021](https://newtongolfinstitute.proboards.com/thread/476/bm-on-ground-reaction-forces)):

> *"Step on the right foot — push up toward the right ear — push off the right foot to get the COP to the left — from the left foot, push up toward the left ear."*

**Why it works:** Each "push toward the ear" creates a GRF vector that is tilted away from vertical — toward the body's center of mass and slightly outward. This angled vector creates the long moment arm that generates rotational torque. The stepping motion prevents the "straight up" push (vertical-dominant without rotation) that can produce early extension or jump without rotation.

**Drill application:** Have the client step in place (step right, step left, continue) at normal walking cadence. Then overlay this cadence onto the golf swing. The golf swing's transition should feel like a weight shift step — COP moves to lead foot before the arms complete the downswing. This is the stepping drill that Kwon also recommends in his YouTube biomechanics sessions ([Kwon YouTube 2022](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=wVC_pFlX5Oc)).

**Observable cue in video:** In face-on video, the pelvis should begin moving toward the target *before* the lead arm reaches arm-parallel in the backswing. When the pelvis move starts after arm-parallel, the downswing is reactive (upper-body initiated) rather than GRF-initiated.

## 8. Kwon + Como + AMG — The Moment Arm Trinity

### 8.1 Three Sources Converging on One Key Variable

Kwon's academic research, Como's applied lab data, and AMG's coaching language all converge on **moment arm length** as the master efficiency variable in GRF mechanics. This consensus is striking because the three sources reach the same conclusion from completely different starting points:

| Source | Starting Point | How They Arrive at Moment Arm |
|---|---|---|
| **Kwon** | 3D inverse dynamics; academic biomechanics | GRF moment = Force × Moment arm; longer arm = more torque for same force magnitude |
| **Como/GASP** | 6DOF force plate measurement | GASP directly calculates moment arm from combined GRF vector direction and COM position |
| **AMG** | Visual coaching analogy (force vector-COM relationship) | "The force vector must stay in front of the COM" = moment arm language without the physics label |

**Teaching the moment arm to clients:** The moment arm is an abstract concept. The three expert sources give three different entry points for clients at different cognitive/learning styles:

| Client Profile | Best Entry Point | Language |
|---|---|---|
| Physics/data orientation | Kwon/Como frame | "The longer the wrench, the more the torque. Your force vector direction determines the wrench length." |
| Visual/kinesthetic | AMG frame | "The force arrow has to aim in front of your body's center. If it aims behind, you're spinning out." |
| Feel-first / intuitive | Koch/Kwon stepping frame | "It feels like a weight-shift step — right side loads, then left side catches and pushes." |

### 8.2 Moment Arm Length — What Makes It Long vs. Short

| Condition | Moment Arm Length | GRF Efficiency |
|---|---|---|
| Force vector tilted horizontally; COM inside the lever | Long | High rotational torque from moderate force |
| Force vector straight vertical; COM on the force line | Short (near zero) | Minimal rotational torque despite large force magnitude |
| Pelvis thrusts toward ball (hip slide) | Short (moment arm collapses) | High force magnitude; low torque output |
| Lead foot pressed into ground with flexed knee; pelvis slightly behind lead foot | Long | Maximum torque leverage |

**The hip-thrust/slide paradox:** Players who are coached to "get into a power position" by sliding the pelvis toward the target often increase total GRF magnitude — the force plate shows a bigger number — but simultaneously collapse the moment arm and reduce rotational torque. This is why some high-force-output players are not as fast as expected: they are pressing harder but pressing in a direction that reduces the lever. Kwon's coaching emphasis on force *direction*, not force *magnitude*, is the corrective principle.

## 9. Motor Learning Communication Philosophy — Winkelman, Analogy, External Focus for GRF Cues

### 9.1 The Winkelman Communication Loop — Applied to GRF

Nick Winkelman's coaching model (from *The Language of Coaching*) provides the structural framework for delivering GRF concepts across a lesson or coaching session ([Some Flow review of Winkelman 2023](https://someflow.substack.com/p/book-review-the-language-of-coaching)):

**DESCRIBE → DEMONSTRATE → CUE → DO → DEBRIEF**

The critical distinction Winkelman makes is between **description** (used outside of performance; verbal explanation of mechanics before the drill) and **cue** (used at the moment of performance; must be short, external-focus, action-triggering).

**GRF-specific application of each step:**

| Winkelman Step | GRF Content | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **DESCRIBE** | Explain the three-force model verbally; use Dahlquist's "three directions" framing; show force trace on Swing Catalyst or pressure mat | "The ground pushes back in three ways — sideways, rotationally, and vertically. Your job is to use them in order." |
| **DEMONSTRATE** | Coach demonstrates step-and-pump drill; shows the stepping rhythm; demonstrates COP transition on pressure mat | Coach steps through the drill at speed with explanation |
| **CUE** | Single external cue, 5 words or fewer | "Stamp your lead heel" / "Twist the wrench" / "Step into the left wall" |
| **DO** | Player executes 5–10 repetitions; coach silent unless safety issue | No new information during DO phase |
| **DEBRIEF** | Compare TrackMan 3D data before/after; check the metric that the drill targeted; ask player for subjective feel | "What did you feel different? The data shows your pelvis sequenced 40 ms earlier." |

**Critical error:** Most GRF instruction fails because coaches compress DESCRIBE and CUE into the same sentence, creating long technical instructions during performance. The player cannot process 25 words while swinging. The cue layer must be **one idea, external, brief**.

### 9.2 External Focus Hierarchy for GRF Cues

Research strongly supports external focus over internal focus for motor skill acquisition and retention ([Wulf 2013; Eggleston, Boise State 2014](https://scholarworks.boisestate.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1878&context=td)). For GRF-specific cues, the hierarchy from most to least external is:

| Cue Level | Example — GRF Concept | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Result/environment (most external)** | "Make the divot point at the flag" (low-point); "Let the ball jump off the face" (speed) | [A] Strongest learning and transfer |
| **Equipment/implement** | "Stamp the pedal into the floor"; "Drag the mat toward you with your heel" | [A/B] Good transfer |
| **Implement-body metaphor** | "Push the ground into the wall"; "Twist the floor under your feet" | [B] Good for intermediate skill |
| **Body-part external** | "Lead heel into the ground"; "Trail foot inside edge" | [B] Acceptable; avoid joint-level (knee, hip) |
| **Body-part internal (avoid in performance)** | "Shift your weight left"; "Extend your lead knee" | [B] Reserve for DESCRIBE phase only |

**Analogy learning — the anti-choke mechanism:** Research on analogy learning in golf shows that players taught with biomechanical metaphors (one analogy encapsulating multiple rules) perform **equally well in training** but **better under pressure** than players taught with explicit technical rules ([Analogy Learning Study, Semanticscholar 2015](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/70cd/d1cd8be76679864b902c54d38726b4e15859.pdf)). The mechanism: analogy-taught players have fewer conscious thoughts; they do not reinvest in technique under pressure because they never stored technique as discrete rules.

**GRF analogy bank (Forefront approved):**

| GRF Concept | Analogy Cue | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Kinetic sequence (shift → twist → jump) | "It's like jumping over a puddle — step, push, spring" | Captures lateral-to-vertical timing without explicit instruction |
| Moment arm / braking | "The longer the wrench, the easier the bolt" (Koch) | Captures the torque lever without physics language |
| Lead-side braking | "Hit the brakes with your lead foot, then spin the wheel" | Captures deceleration → rotation sequence |
| COP-COM distinction | "Lighten the gas pedal, not the car" (AMG) | COP changes fast; COM moves slow |
| Kinetic sequence | "Shift, twist, launch — in order" | Adams' three-word summary of the entire sequence |
| Vertical force timing | "Push the floor away before the ball leaves" | External timing anchor |
| Trail foot loading | "Spike the inside of your right boot into the floor" | External focus; prevents sway |
| X-Factor Stretch | "Let the pelvis leave the party before the shoulders" | Analogy for pelvis-thorax lead |

### 9.3 Staging Communication by Learning Phase

The appropriate **communication style** (explicit technical vs. analogy vs. constraints only) depends on the player's motor learning stage. This maps to Forefront's GP-L stages:

| GP-L Stage | Learning Stage | Appropriate Communication Mode | GRF-Specific Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **GP-L 1** | Cognitive/Verbal (Fitts & Posner Stage 1) | Explicit description; many words allowed; explicit technical rules | Explain three-force model; pressure mat visual; no real-time cues during swings |
| **GP-L 2** | Associative (Stage 2) | Mix explicit and external-focus cues; begin reducing technical description | Use one external cue per block; TrackMan 3D for confirmation; introduce analogies |
| **GP-L 3** | Autonomous onset (Stage 3) | Analogy-primary; constraints; external results only | Remove technical language from cue layer entirely; constraints replace cues |
| **GP-L 4** | Autonomous/Speed | Intent only; no technique cues | "Speed out in front" (Koch); "Make a sound past the ball" — pure intent |
| **GP-L 5** | Performance/Competition | Pre-shot routine only; no GRF language on course | Single process word maximum (e.g., "step" or "launch") |

**The Carson & Collins exception:** There is a legitimate case for **brief internal focus during the earliest stage of a new movement pattern**, specifically when introducing a fundamentally new movement that has no existing motor program to build on ([Carson & Collins 2011 — expert-pattern thesis]). In this case, a short explicit internal description followed immediately by an external analogy is appropriate. Example: "Your trail foot needs to push into the inside edge [internal, one time], like you're pressing a button under the inside of your boot [external analogy]." Once the pattern is partially acquired, drop the internal and retain only the external analogy.

### 9.4 The Feedback Fading Protocol for GRF Technology

Technology (TrackMan 3D, launch monitor, Swing Catalyst) provides augmented feedback. The evidence on feedback frequency strongly favors **fading schedules over constant feedback** for long-term retention ([Schmidt, Lee, Motor Learning textbook; Wulf 2013]):

| Feedback Phase | Schedule | GRF Application |
|---|---|---|
| Early acquisition | After every swing (or nearly so) | Show TrackMan 3D body data after each swing during blocked drill; pressure mat real-time OK |
| Mid-acquisition | Every 3rd–5th swing (33%) | Show data summary after sets; remove from mid-set swings |
| Late acquisition / transfer | Bandwidth (only if metric outside threshold) | Show data only if key metric misses target band |
| Autonomous / competition | Remove or minimize | No TrackMan during transfer or pressure blocks |

**Dependency risk:** Constant TrackMan 3D feedback during practice creates metric-dependency — the player can only self-regulate with the screen. The goal of GRF instruction is not to produce a "good force plate graph" but to produce a player who can self-regulate GRF feel without feedback. The fading schedule is the mechanism.

## 10. P-Position Observation Guide — What to Watch at Each Position for GRF Fault Detection

This section provides **coach-facing observation criteria** at each standard P-position for inferring GRF patterns without a force plate. All observations are based on [B]-level expert canon unless noted.

### 10.1 Address / Setup (P1)

| Observable | Optimal Range | GRF Implication if Outside Range |
|---|---|---|
| Pressure distribution (visual) | Slight favor toward balls of feet; roughly even trail-lead split | Lead-heavy address → Reverse Pivot tendency; trail-heavy → over-sway tendency |
| Knee flex | Moderate athletic flex; not locked; not excessively squatted | Excessive squat at address may produce a flat SSC (no loading room for transition) |
| Foot flare (lead) | 20–35° for most players; biomechanics + body type dependent | Excessive flare delays vertical force; too square may restrict hip rotation |
| Stance width | Roughly shoulder width for driver | Excessively wide reduces lateral shift capacity; too narrow reduces vertical force base |
| Weight distribution (feet view) | Mid-foot to balls of feet; not in heels | Heel-heavy address inhibits vertical push; reduces GRF response in transition |

### 10.2 Mid-Backswing / Arm Parallel (P3)

| Observable | Optimal | GRF Fault Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Pelvis center position | Minimal lateral shift; slight load into trail side | Trail hip moving outside trail foot line → Sway (over-sway/large lateral); no trail shift → pre-Reverse Pivot |
| Trail knee | Maintains flex; slight internal rotation | Trail knee straightening → no trail-side load; COP going to outside trail foot |
| Lead foot | Light (unweighting beginning); does not lift off yet | Lead foot going full weight at P3 → Reverse Pivot; no unweighting → flat SSC |
| Pelvis-thorax separation (face-on) | Slight X-Factor building; pelvis more square, thorax more rotated | Pelvis and thorax turning together → no X-Factor; no GRF loading differential |

### 10.3 Top of Backswing (P4)

| Observable | Optimal | GRF Fault Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Trail-side pressure (estimated from position) | ≥75% trail foot (tour average ~78% driver) | Centered or lead-heavy at top → no trail loading; Reverse Pivot confirmed |
| Trail hip depth | Hip turned, not swayed; hip center over or inside trail foot | Hip outside trail foot → Sway; hip still facing forward → rotation incomplete, forcing OTT |
| Lead foot | Beginning to re-engage (slight pressure returning) | Lead foot still fully unweighted at top with no movement → slow transition timing |
| Pelvis position vs. thorax | Pelvis slightly more open than shoulders; X-Factor maximized | Pelvis and thorax parallel → kinematic sequence disrupted; no GRF-driven differentiation |

### 10.4 Early Transition — P4 to P4.5 (The Most Critical Window)

This is the highest-leverage observation window for GRF fault detection. The transition is where the kinetic sequence begins.

| Observable | Optimal | GRF Fault Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Pelvis direction** | Pelvis begins moving TOWARD target before lead arm begins downswing | Shoulders turn before pelvis begins moving → OTT/upper-body initiation; kinetic sequence reversed |
| **Lead foot contact** | Lead heel contacts ground at or before shoulders begin downswing | Lead heel stamps AFTER shoulders begin → transition is arm/shoulder-initiated; GRF late |
| **X-Factor Stretch** | Pelvis-shoulder separation INCREASES briefly from P4 to early P5 | Separation decreasing immediately → no stretch; gain factor will be low |
| **Trail knee** | Trail knee begins moving toward target (lateral force initiating) | Trail knee staying back → horizontal GRF spike absent or late |

### 10.5 Lead Arm Parallel / P5

| Observable | Optimal | GRF Fault Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Pelvis lead over thorax** | Pelvis clearly open relative to thorax; X-Factor held | Pelvis and thorax aligned at P5 → sequence timing error; rotation is late |
| **Lead leg state** | Knee flexed; beginning extension phase; vertical force rising | Lead knee locked straight too early → vertical force has already peaked (early); or still very flexed → vertical force not yet engaging (late) |
| **Pelvis lateral displacement** | Pelvis has moved 1–2 inches toward target; lead side is accepting weight | Pelvis still lateral trail-side at P5 → late lateral force; mass hasn't transferred |
| **Trail heel state** | Trail heel beginning to lift OR still on ground but weight shifting to forefoot | Trail heel lifting very early at P5 → trail-side force is done; should still have some trail contribution |
| **Hip height relative to address** | Slight downward then upward movement; SSC pattern visible | Hips going sharply up at P5 without prior down → no SSC loading; vertical force pattern skipped |

### 10.6 Impact (P7)

| Observable | Optimal | GRF Fault Signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Lead leg** | Straight or nearly straight; "posted up"; lead hip externally rotated | Bent lead knee at impact → no vertical braking force; handle drag likely |
| **Pelvis position** | Significantly forward/open (35–50° or more relative to target line) | Pelvis square or closed at impact → torque has not cleared; OTT or chicken-wing compensation |
| **Trail foot** | Weight on forefoot/toe; heel significantly elevated; NOT fully off ground for most players | Trail foot flat at impact → no push-off; sequence timing issue |
| **Hip height** | Higher than address (extension has occurred) | Same height or lower than address → no vertical extension force through ball |
| **Head/chest position** | Head slightly back of address ball position; chest staying over ball | Head way forward → pelvis has slid (COM moved; sway gap collapsed); OR head way back → hanging back |
| **Lead shoulder position** | Lead shoulder below trail shoulder (side bend right); not high-leveled | Lead shoulder even or above trail → no side bend; GRF moment arm insufficient to create tilt |

### 10.7 Follow-Through (P8–P10)

Follow-through is a *diagnostic of what happened through impact*, not a position to actively coach. Key GRF inferences:

| Observable | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| Trail foot rotates onto toe and inside edge | AP force couple was active; proper trail-foot rotation through impact |
| Trail heel stays on ground through P8 | Trail-side push-off absent or completed too early |
| Lead leg is fully extended, rigid | Vertical GRF braking occurred properly |
| Lead side buckles (knee bends) in follow-through | Lead-side GRF was insufficient; no real bracing moment |
| Player falls toward target post-impact | Braking force was absent; COM never decelerated |
| Player falls back | Vertical force was up-and-back rather than up-and-through; or braking over-rotation |
