## Executive Summary

The Representative Learning Unit (RLU) concept is **scientifically defensible as a practical dose construct**, but only when it is precisely redefined to align with motor learning theory. The current Forefront draft is directionally correct but under-specified in two critical ways: (1) it conflates a *repetition* with a *learning episode*, and (2) it does not sufficiently distinguish the RLU definition across motor learning stages. The strongest evidence base — contextual interference, the challenge point framework, representative learning design, fatigue-and-consolidation research, and the guidance/feedback hypothesis — all support an intent-gated, perception-action unit rather than a ball-count unit.

**Core recommendation:** Keep the RLU label. Redefine it as a **Resolved Learning Unit** — one complete perception → action → evaluation cycle executed at or above the challenge threshold (65–85% success rate). The word "Representative" is technically accurate but is a domain-specific term that may confuse clients; "Resolved" is marketable, accurate, and explains the *why* instantly. Retain "Representative" as the internal technical descriptor for the acquisition and transfer stages only.

**Key findings at a glance:**

| Question | Answer | Evidence Grade |
|---|---|---|
| Is RLU scientifically defensible? | Yes, if defined as a perception-action-evaluation cycle, not a ball count | Evidence-supported |
| Is 1,000 balls harmful? | Yes — fatigue during skill practice impairs next-session learning ([Branscheidt et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6443347/)) | Evidence-supported |
| What is the optimal challenge point? | 65–85% success rate ([Guadagnoli & Lee, 2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Challenge_point_framework); [Wilson et al., 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6831579/)) | Evidence-supported |
| Should new-pattern cap be 12–18/day coached? | Conservative-to-moderate; supported by fatigue and attentional load evidence | Inferred — review required |
| Are Forefront's client-type adjustments reasonable? | Yes, with calibration for juniors (lower) and seniors (slower rate, not necessarily lower volume) | Forefront-proposed with partial support |
| Should speed/pressure reps be counted separately? | Yes — they are distinct neural demands; separate tracking is appropriate | Evidence-supported |

## 1. Is the RLU Concept Scientifically Defensible?

### 1.1 The Problem With Ball Count

The most common golf practice metric — balls hit — is a measure of *execution rate*, not *learning dose*. Motor learning research makes this distinction repeatedly and emphatically. As the 2024 systematic review of motor learning in golf ([Barzyk & Gruber, *Frontiers in Sports and Active Living*](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10899359/)) concludes, "the better you are, the fewer balls you should hit in succession and/or with the same club." The review synthesizes studies ranging from 60 to 3,000 trials and finds no consistent dose-response curve for ball count — but consistently finds that *schedule, variability, and challenge level* are the operative variables.

The Ericsson et al. deliberate practice framework established that high-quality, effortful, structured practice predicts expert performance far better than gross volume ([Ericsson et al., 1993](https://graphics8.nytimes.com/images/blogs/freakonomics/pdf/DeliberatePractice(PsychologicalReview).pdf)). The subsequent meta-analysis by [Macnamara et al. (2016)](https://hhs.purdue.edu/skill-learning-and-performance-lab/wp-content/uploads/sites/43/2024/08/macnamara-et-al-2016-the-relationship-between-deliberate-practice-and-performance-in-sports-a-meta-analysis.pdf) found that deliberate practice explained only 18% of sports performance variance, meaning effort quality, not hour count or rep count, is the decisive variable. Hitting a bucket of balls mindlessly is not deliberate practice; it is motor groove reinforcement at best and fatigue-induced interference at worst.

### 1.2 The Case For a Qualitative Dose Unit

Several existing constructs provide intellectual ancestors for the RLU:

- **The Plan-Act-Review (PaR) cycle** ([Golf Science Journal](https://www.golfsciencejournal.org/article/5013-par-plan-act-review-golf-motor-learning-research-and-improving-golf-skills.pdf)): one complete deliberate attempt to solve a motor problem, assess the outcome, and update the internal model.
- **The challenge point framework** ([Guadagnoli & Lee, 2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Challenge_point_framework)): learning is maximized when the functional task difficulty sits at the optimal challenge point — too easy, and there is no signal; too hard, and the information is uninterpretable.
- **Representative Learning Design (RLD)** ([Davids et al.](https://canada.humankinetics.com/blogs/excerpt/learning-design-in-ecological-dynamics)): a practice unit must include the perception-action coupling present in the real performance environment to generate meaningful transfer.
- **The OPTIMAL theory** (Wulf & Lewthwaite, 2016): attention, autonomy, and external focus are conditions that must be present for a repetition to count as a learning episode ([Wulf, *Journal of Sport and Exercise Psychology*, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37952707/)).

An RLU, when defined as a **single complete attempt executed with full pre-shot intention, external attentional focus, performance against a defined target, and post-shot evaluation**, is defensible as a first-principles dose unit. It aligns with what the literature calls a "functional learning trial."

### 1.3 What Is Not an RLU

The following should be explicitly excluded from the RLU count:

| Non-RLU activity | Reason to exclude |
|---|---|
| Range warm-up swings without targets | No evaluation loop; no problem to solve |
| Consecutive identical blocked reps past failure plateau | Contextual interference collapses; pattern repetition without cognitive demand |
| Shots hit while fatigued past attentional capacity | Fatigue during skill practice impairs next-session learning ([Branscheidt et al., 2019](https://elifesciences.org/articles/40578)) |
| Shots hit immediately after consulting launch monitor for same metric | Feedback dependency forming; not an independent perception cycle |
| Rehearsal swings or pump swings without ball flight outcome | Kinaesthetic feedback only; no full action-environment interaction |

## 2. Precise RLU Definition

### 2.1 Proposed Definition

> **A Representative Learning Unit (RLU) is one complete, intentional attempt to solve a defined motor problem in golf, comprising: (1) a pre-execution plan that includes target selection, trajectory intent, and shot shape decision; (2) full execution with an external focus of attention; (3) an unassisted evaluation of the outcome against the stated intent; and (4) a brief cognitive update (self-correction, strategy revision, or confirmation). The attempt must occur at or above the challenge threshold (65–85% expected success rate for the current learner-stage combination).**

This definition is grounded in:
- The PaR cycle from applied golf science ([Golf Science Journal](https://www.golfsciencejournal.org/article/5013-par-plan-act-review-golf-motor-learning-research-and-improving-golf-skills.pdf))
- External focus superiority for learning and performance ([Wulf, *Science Direct*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/article/abs/pii/S1469029223001875))
- The challenge point threshold of 65–70% cited in the golf-specific literature ([Golf On Track](https://www.golfontrack.com/post/challenge-point-theory-for-golf); [Golf Science Lab](https://golfwell.co/when-to-use-block-practice-vs-random-practice/))
- The 85% rule for optimal training accuracy in neural learning systems ([Wilson et al., *Nature Communications*, 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6831579/))
- The optimal error rate of ~30% identified empirically for motor tasks ([bioRxiv, 2023](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2023.07.19.549705v1))

### 2.2 RLU Type Classification

Different practice contexts produce different RLU types. The Proposal Engine should track type, not just count.

| RLU Type | Definition | Motor Learning Stage | Counts Toward New-Pattern Cap? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NP-RLU** (New Pattern) | First 10–30 exposures to a specific motor modification | Acquisition (blocked) | Yes — primary limiter |
| **CV-RLU** (Consolidation-Variable) | Variable attempt to express a partially consolidated pattern across contexts | Consolidation (serial) | Partial — 0.5 weight toward cap |
| **CI-RLU** (Contextual Interference) | Random-order attempt across 3+ task variations | Transfer (random/representative) | No — total session cap applies |
| **RP-RLU** (Representative / On-Course) | Full shot simulation with course-relevant constraints, one ball per scenario | Representative / Transfer | No — counted as session RLU |
| **PR-RLU** (Pressure) | High-stakes attempt after consolidation confirmed; scored or consequence-bearing | Pressure / Maintenance | Separate cap (4–8/session) |
| **SP-RLU** (Speed / Overspeed) | Maximum-intent velocity attempt (SuperSpeed, light implement) | Power acquisition | Separate cap (18–36/session) |
| **MN-RLU** (Maintenance) | Rehearsal of consolidated pattern during in-season / competition week | Maintenance | Minimal — 4–10/session |

## 3. Evidence-Supported Volume Bands by Motor Learning Stage

### 3.1 Stage Definitions

| Stage | Description | Key Research Insight |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition** | New pattern introduction; blocked practice appropriate for novices | Distributed outperforms massed 4:1 (60 trials/4 days vs. 240/1 day, [Dail & Christina, 2004](https://ojs.ub.uni-konstanz.de/cpa/article/view/5306/4878)) |
| **Consolidation** | Pattern stabilizing; moving from blocked to serial; internal-to-external focus shift | Sleep consolidation requires 24h; no within-12h additional practice benefit ([Oxford Sleep, 2018](https://academic.oup.com/sleep/article/41/9/zsy123/5042787)) |
| **Retention** | Pattern available under consistent conditions; maintenance focus | Spacing effect: 4 × 50-min sessions outperform 1 × 200-min session ([VirTra](https://www.virtra.com/the-spacing-effect-in-skills-training-and-deliberate-practice/)) |
| **Transfer** | Pattern expressed in varied/representative conditions | Contextual interference group superior at transfer ([Barzyk & Gruber, 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10899359/)) |
| **Competitive Maintenance** | Pattern robust under pressure; reinvestment managed | Choking linked to self-focus reinvestment; pressure acclimatization required ([Wells & Skowronski, 2012](https://www.niu.edu/jskowronski/publications/2012WellsSkowronski.pdf)) |

**Evidence grade key:** ✅ Evidence-supported | ⚠️ Inferred — review required | 🔵 Forefront-proposed

| Motor Learning Stage | Minimum Effective Dose (daily) | Target Dose (daily) | Upper Useful Dose (daily) | Point of Diminishing Returns | Evidence Grade |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Acquisition (new pattern, NP-RLU) | 8 NP-RLU | 12–16 NP-RLU | 18–20 NP-RLU | >20 NP-RLU | ⚠️ |
| Consolidation (CV-RLU) | 10 CV-RLU | 15–20 CV-RLU | 25 CV-RLU | >30 CV-RLU | ⚠️ |

| Speed / Overspeed (SP-RLU) | 20 SP-RLU per session | 24–30 SP-RLU | 36 SP-RLU | >40 SP-RLU | ✅ (overspeed lit.) |

**Rationale for acquisition cap at 18–20 NP-RLU:**  
Lab studies on golf motor learning used between 60 and 288 total trials for entire multi-week protocols ([Barzyk & Gruber, 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10899359/)). At 4–6 days/week that implies 10–72 trials/day, but with distributed practice outperforming massed at ratios of 4:1 or higher. Fatigue research ([Branscheidt et al., 2019](https://elifesciences.org/articles/40578)) shows that practicing in a fatigued state produces motor memories that impair next-session learning — fatigued subjects needed four additional days to catch up with non-fatigued subjects who learned the skill fresh. Given that each NP-RLU demands full attentional engagement and cognitive evaluation, 18–20 NP-RLU is estimated to represent approximately 40–60 minutes of genuine skill work including inter-trial reflection. Beyond this range, attentional quality degrades and the guidance hypothesis risks activating: frequent feedback produces dependency rather than independent error detection ([Schmidt, 1991, via NAU](https://experts.nau.edu/en/publications/effects-of-physical-guidance-and-knowledge-of-results-on-motor-le/)).

Seniors may benefit from shorter but more frequent sessions rather than reduced total weekly volume.

## 6. Why 1,000 Balls Is Not the Answer: The Evidence Case

This section provides the scientific basis for what is simultaneously Forefront's strongest marketing argument and most important coaching safeguard.

### 6.1 Fatigue Corrupts Motor Memory Formation

[Branscheidt et al. (2019, *eLife*)](https://elifesciences.org/articles/40578) conducted four experiments demonstrating that practicing a force-control motor skill while fatigued produces detrimental effects that persist across *subsequent non-fatigued sessions*. Key numbers:

- Fatigued participants achieved only **68% of the performance level** of non-fatigued participants at the end of session 2.
- The detrimental effect persisted for **almost two complete additional training sessions** before equalizing.
- The mechanism is *centrally mediated* — corrupted motor memory formation in M1, not simply peripheral muscle failure.
- Fatigue during training produced memories "optimal for the fatigued state" that actively interfered with fresh-state learning.

**Translation to 1,000 balls:** At ~60–80 balls per hour on a range, 1,000 balls represents 12–17 hours of continuous practice, or more commonly a 3–4 hour session of 250–350 balls. At this volume, attentional and physical fatigue are inevitable. Every rep taken in a fatigued state deposits a corrupted motor memory. The golfer leaves the range having worked hard and having learned poorly.

### 6.2 Massed Practice Fails on Retention and Transfer

The most replicated finding in golf motor learning is that distributed practice (60 trials over 4 days) outperforms massed practice (240 trials in one day) at delayed retention tests ([Dail & Christina, 2004, via University of Konstanz review](https://ojs.ub.uni-konstanz.de/cpa/article/view/5306/4878)). The massed group looked better *during* practice — a classic performance vs. learning dissociation. Four weeks later, the distributed group retained significantly more.

The spacing effect is one of the most replicated findings in cognitive and motor learning: four sessions of 50 minutes each outperforms one session of 200 minutes ([VirTra](https://www.virtra.com/the-spacing-effect-in-skills-training-and-deliberate-practice/)). Clinically, spaced training in surgical skill acquisition shows dramatically superior long-term retention compared to massed training of equivalent total time.

### 6.3 Blocked Massed Practice Builds the Wrong Memory

Random practice outperforms blocked practice at retention and transfer in experienced golfers, even though blocked practice produces better performance *during* the session ([Barzyk & Gruber, 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10899359/)). Hitting 100 7-irons to the same flag builds a single rigid motor program useful only in that condition. The golfer on the course never faces the same shot twice. Motor schemas — the adaptable rules that enable transfer — are built through variability, not repetition ([Rifton Motor Skills Learning](https://www.rifton.com/education-center/articles/motor-skills-learning-practice-part-iii)).

### 6.4 Attentional Degradation Reduces All Reps Past a Threshold to Zero

Attention is the gating variable for motor learning. The K-Motion applied model states explicitly: "Hitting your favorite club 50 times in a row to the same target is not a desired environment; doing so fails to stimulate cognitive stress, and zero learning takes place" ([K-Motion](https://www.k-motion.com/the-learning-performance-continuum-how-k-motion-has-helped-our-students-retain-and-transfer-golfing-skills/)). Optimal spacing within a session — "20 balls in 20 minutes" rather than 20 balls in 5 minutes — maintains the cognitive stress that drives deeper encoding. The spacing effect operates within a session, not just between sessions.

### 6.5 Feedback Dependency: The Launch Monitor Problem

Augmented feedback is beneficial for learning — but only when faded. When provided at high frequency for every trial, augmented feedback (including launch monitor data after every shot) produces **guidance dependency**: the learner uses the external feedback as a replacement for intrinsic error detection rather than developing their own perception-action calibration ([Schmidt, 1991, via NAU](https://experts.nau.edu/en/publications/effects-of-physical-guidance-and-knowledge-of-results-on-motor-le/)). In competition, the launch monitor disappears. The golfer who required ball speed and spin rate after every shot has no internal reference. A **technology fading protocol** — present during acquisition, faded during consolidation, absent during representative and pressure stages — is not optional; it is neurologically required for durable skill.

### 6.6 The 1,000-Ball Session Visualized

A golfer hitting 1,000 balls in one session with a driver experiences approximately:

- **Rep 1–50:** Productive acquisition (assuming external focus, challenge point met) — legitimate RLU zone
- **Rep 51–150:** Quality degrades as attentional resources partially exhaust; beginning of massed practice penalty
- **Rep 151–400:** Pattern repetition without cognitive signal; range of no meaningful learning increment
- **Rep 401–700:** Fatigue state established; motor memories formed here are detrimental to next session
- **Rep 701–1,000:** Force production inconsistent; kinaesthetic feedback corrupted; motor engram contaminated

Legitimate RLU count from a 1,000-ball session: **~15–40 NP-RLU** (those executed in the first 30–60 minutes at full attentional engagement). Ball count: 1,000. Efficiency rate: 1.5–4%.

### 7.2 Cue Fading Protocol

Cue fading is required to prevent guidance dependency from verbal coaching cues as much as from technology feedback. Recommended sequence:

1. **Acquisition (weeks 1–2):** Full verbal cue with each NP-RLU. Internal → external focus transition as pattern stabilizes.
2. **Consolidation (weeks 3–4):** Cue fading begins. Coach provides cue every 3rd CV-RLU. Learner self-cues on intermediate reps.
3. **Transfer:** No verbal cue during execution. Pre-set briefing only. Post-set debrief.
4. **Representative/Pressure:** Golfer self-initiates all cues. Coach is silent during execution. External focus instruction embedded in pre-round routine only.

**Research basis:** External focus of attention is consistently superior to internal focus for both performance and learning in golf ([Wulf, *PubMed*, 2024](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37952707/)). Any reference to body parts during execution constrains the motor system and introduces co-contractions that degrade fluency and accuracy. The practical implication: coach cues should refer to target, ball flight, or club path *outcome* — never joint angles, positions, or muscle activation.

### 7.3 The 65–85% Challenge Zone

The challenge point framework ([Guadagnoli & Lee, 2004](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Challenge_point_framework)) predicts that the optimal learning point is the task difficulty at which the learner generates interpretable information — enough challenge to require effort, not so much that the error signal is uninterpretable. For golf, this translates to:

- A beginner should fail 15–35% of shots (success rate 65–85%)
- An experienced golfer learning a new pattern should fail 20–30% (success rate 70–80%)
- A competitive golfer in transfer practice should fail 20–25% (success rate 75–80%)

The [85% Rule for optimal learning](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6831579/) (Wilson et al., *Nature Communications*, 2019) and [empirical evidence of ~30% optimal error rate for motor tasks](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2023.07.19.549705v1) converge on this range. The Forefront AMG checkpoint score provides the natural data source for challenge-point calibration.

### 8.3 Progression Gate Criteria

| Gate Type | Criteria | Proposal Engine Action |
|---|---|---|
| **NP → CV Progression Gate** | ≥70% success rate on 3 consecutive NP-RLU sessions; pattern reproducible without verbal cue | Unlock CV-RLU; reduce NP-RLU prescription; introduce 2nd variation |
| **CV → CI Progression Gate** | ≥70% retention test score after 48h sleep gap; no cue-dependent regression | Unlock CI-RLU; initiate KR fading protocol |
| **CI → RP Progression Gate** | ≥75% success rate in random practice; transfer test (different context) shows ≥65% retention | Unlock RP-RLU; transition to on-course simulation |
| **RP → PR Progression Gate** | On-course simulation score within 10% of flat-lie range score; 3+ retention tests passed | Unlock PR-RLU; introduce stakes-based practice |
| **Regression Gate** | Any drop of >20% from gate test score; or pain flag; or readiness RED | Regress one stage; reduce NP-RLU prescription; mandatory 48h rest + assessment |

### 8.4 Daily Session Dose Resolver Logic

The resolver should apply this hierarchy for each session type:

```
Session Dose = f(client_type, motor_learning_stage, session_duration, readiness_score)

1. Readiness gate: if readiness RED → set all caps to 0; stop.
2. Session duration sets base capacity (see Section 4 table).
3. Motor learning stage determines RLU type weights:
   - Acquisition: 70% NP-RLU, 20% CV-RLU, 10% warm-up
   - Consolidation: 40% NP-RLU, 40% CV-RLU, 20% CI-RLU
   - Transfer: 10% NP-RLU, 30% CV-RLU, 60% CI-RLU
   - Representative: 0% NP-RLU, 20% CV-RLU, 80% RP-RLU
   - Pressure: 0% NP-RLU, 10% MN-RLU, 90% PR-RLU
4. Apply client-type cap from Section 8.2.
5. Apply fatigue gate: if prior session was same-day and duration >45 min → reduce NP-RLU by 50%.
6. Apply challenge calibration: if AMG checkpoint score indicates <65% → reduce NP-RLU; increase blocked CV-RLU.
7. Output: {session_type, total_rlu, np_rlu, cv_rlu, ci_rlu, rp_rlu, pr_rlu, sp_rlu, challenge_band, coach_notes}
```

## 9. Client-Facing and Marketing Language

### 9.1 Core Concept Explanation (Plain Language)

**"What is an RLU?"**

> An RLU — a Resolved Learning Unit — is one complete, intentional shot attempt. Not every swing counts as one. A swing counts as an RLU only when you had a specific plan before you swung, a specific target and shot shape in mind, and you evaluated what happened afterward without checking a screen. One genuine attempt like this teaches your brain more than fifty mindless range swings.

**"Why not just count balls?"**

> Ball count tells you how much you swung. RLU count tells you how much you learned. A golfer can hit 300 balls in an hour and accumulate fewer learning units than someone who hit 15 balls with full intentional practice. We measure what the brain actually processes — not what the bucket contained.

**For clients who love volume:**

Language teachers know that 20 minutes of focused, conversational practice beats four hours of passive listening. Your brain's motor system works exactly the same way. We give you the equivalent of intensive language lessons — not a six-hour lecture."

**For the data-minded golfer:**

**For competitive golfers:**

> "Your shot on hole 14 of a tournament is never the same as the shot on hole 14 of your last practice session. Your brain needs to learn how to solve *novel* golf problems, not just repeat one solution. RLU-based practice builds adaptable motor schemas. Ball-count practice builds fragile, context-dependent habits that break under pressure."

### 9.4 Coach Script: Explaining RLU to a Client

> "Here's how I think about your practice. Every time you step up to a ball with a clear plan — target picked, shot shape decided, full commitment — and then you evaluate honestly what happened, that's one learning unit. That one rep teaches your brain something real. 
>
> When you hit 200 balls at the range, you might get 15 or 20 of those genuine learning reps in the first hour. After that, your attention fades, your body gets tired, and your brain actually starts encoding the *tired* version of your swing. You leave the range having worked hard and having reinforced some patterns you don't want.
>
> So here's what we're going to do. I'm going to give you a target: 12 intentional attempts today. Not 12 balls — 12 complete learning units. Each one with a plan, a commitment, and an honest evaluation. Then we stop. You'll feel like we barely practiced. But in 48 hours, when your brain has had a chance to process, you will have learned more than from two buckets of mindless range balls."

### 9.5 FAQ

**Q: How many balls will I hit per RLU session?**  
A: It depends on your stage and session type. In an acquisition session, you might hit 12–20 quality attempts plus warm-up and rest intervals — perhaps 25–40 physical swings total. In a transfer session, each RLU is a unique problem, so variety is higher and total ball count may be similar but every ball serves a different purpose.

**Q: Can I do more RLUs if I feel good?**  
A: Feeling good during practice is one of the most misleading signals in motor learning. The period of feeling "dialed in" often corresponds to a plateau where you're executing a practiced pattern — not learning a new one. Forefront's dose cap is not about how tired you feel; it's about how much your brain can actually encode before the learning window closes.

**Q: Does this mean I should never hit a lot of balls?**  
A: Not exactly. High-volume practice has its place in two scenarios: (1) physical warm-up and general groove maintenance, and (2) speed training, where the volume serves a specific neuromuscular loading purpose. What the evidence is clear about is that high-volume *new-pattern practice* in a single session is counterproductive. Forefront separates these into different RLU types and prescribes each appropriately.

**Q: What about putting? I can make 100 putts in 20 minutes.**  
A: The research is specific here: distributed putting practice (60 attempts over 4 days) dramatically outperforms massed putting (240 attempts in one day) at 28-day retention tests. Quantity of putts dropped is not the measure — variety of reads, distances, and green slopes is. Forefront's putting prescription is structured for representative diversity, not volume accumulation.

## 11. Conclusion and Final Verdict

### 11.1 Should Forefront Keep, Rename, Redefine, or Replace RLU?

**Verdict: Keep the RLU construct. Redefine the acronym and tighten the definition. Rename for marketing.**

| Decision | Recommendation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Keep the concept | ✅ Yes | The perception-action-evaluation cycle is scientifically valid and unique to Forefront's positioning |
| Keep "RLU" as internal label | ✅ Yes | Aligns with existing schema; familiar to team |
| Rename for marketing | ✅ Yes — "Resolved Learning Unit" | "Representative" is a technical motor learning term; "Resolved" is intuitive, implies completion, and communicates the evaluation step |
| Tighten the definition | ✅ Mandatory | Current definition is too broad; must include challenge threshold, external focus, unassisted evaluation, and cognitive update as required components |
| Add RLU type taxonomy | ✅ Yes | NP / CV / CI / RP / PR / SP / MN types are necessary for accurate dose prescription across stages |
| Replace with ball count | ❌ No | Ball count is scientifically indefensible as a learning dose unit |
| Replace with time | ❌ No | Time is correlated but not causally linked to learning; a 60-minute unfocused session and a 20-minute high-quality session are not equivalent |

### 11.2 The Three Non-Negotiable Design Principles

1. **An RLU must include evaluation.** A swing without an honest post-shot assessment is not a learning unit. This is the single most important distinction from ball count.

2. **RLU count must be stage-specific.** A new-pattern RLU (NP-RLU) during acquisition and a pressure RLU (PR-RLU) during competitive simulation are neurologically distinct events and must be tracked and capped separately.

3. **Volume must be capped at the attentional and fatigue threshold, not at the willingness threshold.** Clients who want to practice more must be educated — through the evidence and through the marketing language — that more is not more past the productive zone. The Proposal Engine should make this automatic and non-negotiable.

### 11.4 Priority Research Gaps

The following gaps exist in the literature and are areas where Forefront can develop proprietary data through telemetry:

1. **Exact NP-RLU caps by level:** No study has directly tested daily new-pattern acquisition volume as a capped variable in golf. The proposed caps are derived from session volumes in multi-day distributed practice studies. Internal telemetry linking AMG checkpoint score improvement to NP-RLU volume per session would be groundbreaking.

2. **Technology fading dose-response in golf:** The guidance hypothesis is established conceptually, but the optimal fading schedule for launch monitor feedback in golf skill acquisition is unstudied. Forefront's data could establish the first evidence-based protocol.

3. **RLU dose for senior populations:** The aging motor learning literature confirms capacity for learning across the lifespan but lacks golf-specific volume recommendations. Forefront's senior client base is an opportunity for differentiated research.

4. **Cross-goal RLU type interactions:** Whether concurrent physical training (GP-02 strength/power) affects the daily NP-RLU capacity for technical skill work (GP-06 acquisition) is an open question with clear relevance to the concurrent training constraints already referenced in the GTR system.
